## Chronology-First Treatment

The page now starts with a chronology of released, declassified, and publicly
accessible document controls. This is the lead compiler move: establish date
sequence first, then decide whether the document belongs to Volume I, needs a
source packet, or should be handed off to a regional or functional volume.

Diary entries should be cited in working notes as source-trail evidence:
repository, collection/office, diary file unit, NAID, date, and schedule-control
status. They should not become final FRUS documentary evidence without a
corresponding substantive record.

## Source-Note Style Check

The candidate-record cards now use FRUS-style source-note targets. The working
pattern is: `Source:` repository, collection or office, file unit or directive,
date when known; then classification, distribution, copy status, drafting, or
verification status. Precedent checks:

- FRUS 1977-1980, Volume I, Document 5 leads its source note with the Carter
  Library, donated-material collection, file, box, chron folder, and
  classification status.
- FRUS 1981-1988, Volume I, Document 36 leads with the Department of State
  Executive Secretariat file, lot number, chron folder, classification, drafter,
  and copy status.
- FRUS 1969-1976, Volume XXXVIII, Part 1, Document 1 leads with National
  Archives/Nixon Presidential Materials, office files, box, folder,
  classification, and copy status.

For Clinton candidates, final source notes must not be treated as finished
until a document-level pull supplies exact folder, date, classification,
copy/version, and release-status information. Public indexes, archived web
texts, and finding aids remain locators unless paired with the underlying
record.

## Precedent Document Types

Previous Foundations and Nixon-Ford companion volumes broaden the candidate
universe beyond published strategy reports:

- FRUS 1969-1976, Volume I includes public addresses, a public article, an
  essay, presidential letters, memoranda to and from White House principals,
  Cabinet-level reporting, special messages to Congress, and editorial notes.
- FRUS 1969-1976, Volume XXXVIII, Part 1 includes background briefings,
  White House tapes, President/Kissinger memoranda of conversation, Cabinet
  and NSC minutes, NSSM/NSDM files, annual reports, speech files, and editorial
  notes.
- FRUS 1977-1980, Volume I includes campaign and pre-inaugural speeches,
  transition priority memoranda, Vice Presidential travel speeches, letters to
  foreign leaders, memoranda for the record, congressional testimony, Policy
  Planning action memoranda, NSC weekly reporting, Policy Review Committee
  minutes, and editorial notes.
- FRUS 1981-1988, Volume I includes campaign statements, transition-team
  reports, advisory-board papers, presidential speeches, draft speech files,
  NSC and Executive Secretariat records, Policy Planning memoranda, briefing
  books, talking points, congressional records, and editorial notes.

For the Clinton volume, the practical candidate types are:

1. Delivered public doctrine speeches and presidential statements.
2. Background and on-background press briefings by senior principals.
3. Campaign and transition statements that explain later governing premises.
4. Published articles, essays, and intellectual frame-setters by principals.
5. Transition-team, advisory-board, and first-six-month priority papers.
6. Presidential letters and messages to Cabinet officers, Congress, and foreign leaders.
7. Memoranda to or from the President, National Security Advisor, Secretary of State, and Policy Planning.
8. NSC, Cabinet, Principals, Deputies, PRC, and interagency meeting minutes.
9. Congressional testimony, hearing records, and special messages.
10. Briefing books, talking points, draft speeches, press guidance, and rollout papers.
11. Daily diary, trip, schedule, and evening-report chronology controls.
12. Editorial notes for unavailable directive texts, source clusters, and adjacent-volume handoffs.

## Selection Rule

Use a candidate in Volume I only when it helps explain the administration's
foundational foreign-policy framework, interagency policy process, public
doctrine, or high-level strategy. If the record primarily documents NATO
enlargement, Russia, the Balkans, arms control, counterterrorism, foreign
economic policy, or another functional implementation field, use it only as a
cross-reference and route the detailed record to the adjacent topical volume.
